01The First-Principles Primer

Before any financial analysis: what is the home-based DME business, what is "Patient Direct", and how does Accendra make money? If you've never thought about how the CPAP machine in your uncle's bedroom got billed, start here.

01

What is "DME"?

Durable Medical Equipment = physical medical gear that's prescribed for use at home and lasts more than 3 years. Think: CPAP machines, oxygen concentrators, hospital beds, wheelchairs, insulin pumps, glucose monitors, ostomy bags, catheters, wound-care supplies.

The U.S. DME market is ~$70B and growing 6% per year, with specialized home-care sub-segments expanding 10–12% as care shifts out of hospitals.

02

Who's in the Loop?

It's a four-sided market. (1) Manufacturer (ResMed, Philips, Dexcom, Abbott) makes the device. (2) Doctor writes a prescription. (3) DME provider (Accendra) fulfils it — delivers gear, sets it up, supplies consumables, handles compliance. (4) Payer (Medicare, Medicaid, commercial insurer) reimburses.

Patients almost never choose. The DME provider is picked by the doctor's referral or the payer's network.

03

How Does Accendra Bill?

Fee-for-service: Bill the payer per episode of care — equipment rental, monthly resupplies, setup visits. Each chronic patient is a recurring annuity.

Capitation: A health plan pays a fixed $/member/month to handle all DME needs of a patient pool. Lower margin but more predictable. The contract Accendra just lost was a capitation deal worth $300M of revenue.

04

Why "Recurring Revenue"?

Diabetes, sleep apnea, COPD, ostomy — these don't go away. 85% of Accendra's annual revenue comes from existing patients who reorder month after month, year after year. CPAP supplies (mask, hose, filter) refresh every 90 days. Insulin pump supplies every 30 days. Once a patient is on Accendra's books, they stay for years.

This is the closest thing to a SaaS-like model in healthcare distribution.

05

Razor & Blades

Apria's CPAP and respiratory business is a classic razor/blades model. The device (CPAP, ventilator, oxygen concentrator) is rented to the patient — modest margins. The consumables (masks, tubing, filters, oxygen) reorder for years — high margins.

This is why GLP-1 drugs that "cure" sleep apnea threaten the model: shorter therapy = fewer reorders.

06

Why the Industry Is Hard

Three structural challenges:

  • Reimbursement complexity: 650+ contracts, every payer has different rules, denials and audits routine.
  • Regulatory: CMS DMEPOS Competitive Bidding can slash rates 50% on a category overnight.
  • Capital intensive: $160-170M of capex/year goes into rented patient equipment.

A Day in the Life of an Accendra Revenue Dollar

Step 1 — Referral

A pulmonologist at a hospital diagnoses moderate-to-severe sleep apnea. The discharge planner needs to send the patient home with a CPAP. They call Apria — a "national preferred provider" in the hospital's network.

Step 2 — Verify

Apria's automated payor qualification system runs the patient's insurance in seconds. Coverage confirmed. A respiratory therapist is dispatched to the patient's home with the prescribed AirSense 11.

Step 3 — Setup & Bill

Therapist sets up the device, fits the mask, walks through compliance. Apria bills the payer for the device rental + setup visit. Patient enrolls in monthly resupply program.

Step 4 — Recurring

Mask + hose + filter ship every 90 days. Compliance is monitored remotely (CMS requires usage data). My Apria app pings the patient. Revenue stream lives 5+ years if the patient sticks.

02The Business Model

Two go-to-market brands, one operational backbone. After the divestiture, every revenue dollar flows through one of two engines: Apria (high-touch device & respiratory) or Byram (e-commerce style chronic-care consumables).

Apria — The Respiratory & Sleep Specialist

Acquired in 2022 for $1.57B. Generates the majority of Accendra's revenue. National leader in three clinical categories:

  • Sleep Therapy: CPAP and BiPAP. ~$737M revenue (27% of total). Razor/blades model — device rental + 90-day mask/hose resupply.
  • Home Respiratory: Stationary & portable oxygen, non-invasive ventilation (NIV) for COPD. ~$440M (16%).
  • Negative Pressure Wound Therapy: National scale provider.

The differentiator: A network of in-home respiratory therapists who actually visit patients to set up gear, train them, and monitor compliance. This is the moat — commoditized fulfillment houses don't have boots on the ground. Hospital discharge planners pick Apria because they don't want a delayed discharge or a re-admission.

Byram — The Chronic-Care E-Commerce Engine

Acquired in 2017 for $380M at 10.6x EBITDA. Operates like a healthcare-specific Amazon for recurring chronic-care supplies. Almost all revenue is repeat orders by patients managing lifelong conditions.

  • Diabetes: ~$796M (29% of total). Insulin pumps, continuous glucose monitors (CGMs), test strips. Largest category.

The reach: Byram services 85% of U.S. covered lives with 650+ payer contracts and thousands of plan types. ByramConnect — an AI-powered patient platform — aggregates data from CGMs, blood-pressure monitors, fitness trackers and feeds insights back to care teams. Sticky.

Revenue Recognition

Two contract types:

  • Fee-for-service (~majority): Bill payer per episode. Revenue recognized at expected reimbursement (not list price).
  • Capitation: Fixed $/patient/month — Accendra takes utilization risk. Lower margin, more predictable.

Cash conversion: working-capital intensive. DSO of 12–20 days, but denial rates and audit recoupments make collections messy.

Cost Structure

  • COGS ~53%: Cost of devices, consumables, freight.
  • SG&A ~37%: Distribution centers (300+), respiratory therapists, billing/claims operations, IT.
  • Capex ~$160-170M/yr: Mostly rented patient equipment (CPAPs, oxygen concentrators, NIV).
  • D&A ~5% of rev: Heavy because of equipment fleet.

Operating leverage is moderate — fixed costs (rent, IT, billing ops) ~25% of total. Each 100 bps of EBITDA margin = ~$26M of additional EBITDA, ~$0.24 EPS.

05Competitive Moat & Position

AlphaSense scores Accendra at 11.5/16 (72%) "Above Average" quality. The moat is real but narrower than peers like AdaptHealth (4.2M patients, $3.2B revenue, $1.8B mkt cap). Three pillars hold it up; three vulnerabilities threaten it.

01

National Scale & Single-Source Capability

One of only ~3 platforms (with AdaptHealth, Lincare) able to act as a national single-vendor for payers and IDNs across multiple chronic categories. Optum named Apria & Byram preferred DME providers. Hospital discharge planners want one number to call, not five.

02

Reimbursement & Payer Connectivity

650+ payer contracts, services 85% of U.S. covered lives. Automated payor qualification systems cut order turnaround from 30–60 days to ~7 days. This is a tech-enabled compliance moat — the rules get harder every year, scaled players absorb cost better.

03

Recurring Revenue & Patient Stickiness

85% of annual revenue from existing patients. CPAP supplies refresh every 90 days, insulin pump supplies every 30 days. Once a patient is set up at home, switching is a hassle they avoid. Apria's RT network adds clinical service that commoditized players can't match.

04

Technology Stack — ByramConnect & My Apria

ByramConnect is an AI-powered patient platform aggregating CGM, BP-cuff, fitness data and feeding it back to care teams (population-level reporting). My Apria patient app launches Q2 2026 to drive CPAP adherence. Both serve as integration moats with payers and providers.

!1

Supplier Concentration

Top 3 manufacturers account for ~40% of equipment & supply purchases. ResMed, Dexcom, etc., have leverage. Limits Accendra's ability to absorb manufacturer cost increases — and 2026 EBITDA guide explicitly cites manufacturer cost inflation as a margin headwind.

!2

Locked Out of Acute Care

By divesting hospital distribution, Accendra loses the bundle. Mega-distributors Medline, Cardinal, McKesson use comprehensive prime-vendor agreements with hospitals to push their own private-label home-care products through. Former O&M GVP: this is the biggest long-term risk.

!3

Pricing Power Limited

~45% of revenue is government-funded (Medicare + Medicaid + MA). 2026 CMS competitive bidding adds CGMs/pumps to bid categories with target Jan 2028 rates. Prior bidding rounds cut some category rates ~50%. Margins ~10% vs high-teens for diversified peers.

!4

GLP-1 Disruption Risk to CPAP

Sleep apnea = 27% of revenue. Tirzepatide FDA-approved for OSA Dec 2024. Komodo Health data: tirzepatide patients started CPAP 83% less than non-GLP-1 patients. Even if mainstream adoption is 5+ years out, it shortens the duration of the high-margin resupply annuity.

06Industry Tailwinds & Crosswinds

Two forces pull Accendra's market upward; two structural headwinds threaten unit economics. Understanding both is essential to underwriting the thesis.

▲ Tailwind 1: Demographic Wave

  • 71M Americans will be 65+ by 2030 (1 in 5)
  • 40% of U.S. adults live with ≥1 chronic condition
  • 12% of U.S. adults have ≥5 chronic conditions
  • 37M+ diagnosed diabetics + 96M prediabetics
  • 5 of top-10 causes of death are preventable/treatable chronic disease

Chronic patients = recurring revenue. Aging = volume growth. Math compounds.

▲ Tailwind 2: Site-of-Care Shift

  • Home care is 30–50% cheaper than hospital/SNF
  • Payers & MA plans actively incentivize home-based care
  • Home-care market growing 10–12% annually (specialty)
  • DME market: $70B → ~$94B by 2030 (6% CAGR)
  • CMS frees hospital capacity for acute cases

CEO Pesicka: "The home has become an essential care setting, supporting longer, healthier lives for patients and unlocking greater efficiency."

▼ Crosswind 1: GLP-1 Disruption

The threat to Apria's $737M sleep franchise. December 2024: FDA approved Eli Lilly's tirzepatide (Zepbound) for moderate-to-severe OSA in adults with obesity. Komodo Health data:

  • OSA patients on tirzepatide start CPAP 83% less than non-GLP-1 patients
  • Among new OSA pts: only 4% on tirzepatide started CPAP vs 22% otherwise
  • JAMA NetOpen: GLP-1 users 8% less likely to need CPAP at 1-yr follow-up

Counterpoint: >50% of U.S. OSA cases are still undiagnosed. Even halting new CPAP starts wouldn't immediately collapse the resupply annuity — base of 1M+ existing CPAP users continues.

▼ Crosswind 2: CMS Competitive Bidding

The threat to diabetes and ostomy categories. CMS finalized DMEPOS rule in late 2025:

  • CGMs + insulin pumps reclassified to "frequent & substantial servicing" (monthly rental basis)
  • Diabetes, ostomy, urology added to bid categories
  • Bidding occurs late 2026; new rates effective Jan 1, 2028
  • Prior rounds cut some categories ~50%

Counterpoint: Bidding favors scaled, compliant operators. Smaller regional DMEs likely consolidate — Accendra could gain volume share even if price/unit falls. Magnitude of net impact unknowable until bids submitted.

Discount Factor 2

Lost contract creates 2026 trough. Removes after Q1 2027 lap.

Discount Factor 3

Stranded costs of $36.5M. Phased to ~$10M by 2027.

If any one overhang clears, multiple should expand by 1-2 turns. If all three clear, 7–9x is realistic — implying $13-17 share price as in the bull/base cases above. The market doesn't need to love ACH; it just needs to stop fearing bankruptcy.

10Bull vs Bear — The Live Debate

Every position needs a steel-manned counter. AlphaSense, Deutsche Bank fixed-income, Fitch, Jefferies, and a half-dozen industry experts have all weighed in. Here's what each side actually believes.

Liquidity is Sufficient — Bankruptcy Priced In, Won't Happen

$282M cash + $217M revolver + $150M securitization + 2026 FCF $90-110M + retained NOLs from divestiture = clear path through both 2026 and 2027 maturities, even under stress. Market cap of $281M implies bankruptcy probability that doesn't square with the math.

Pure-Play Margin Story Improves Through 2027

Stranded costs phase from $36.5M to ~$10M by 2027 (+$25M EBITDA). Debt paydown saves ~$20M annual interest. Lost contract laps Q1 2027. Replaced with higher-margin business. Each lever individually worth 80-100bps of EBITDA margin — combined could lift margins from 13.2% to ~14-15%.

Demographic Tailwinds Are Real and Compounding

71M Americans age 65+ by 2030. 96M prediabetic. Home-care market growing 10-12%. Once a CPAP or insulin pump patient enters Accendra's funnel they're a 5-10 year annuity. Even modest organic growth +3% on the existing base lifts revenue $80M/year.

Capital Allocation Has Pivoted Constructively

The Rotech acquisition collapse, while costly ($80M fee), was a blessing — it forced the divestiture conversation and avoided doubling down on CPAP just before the GLP-1 wave. Stock rose 14% on Rotech termination. Mgmt now firmly anti-M&A, all FCF earmarked for debt.

Optionality on Optum National Preferred Agreement

The recently secured nationwide preferred-provider agreement with Optum (UnitedHealth) provides volume backstop and improves replaceability of the lost capitation contract. If Optum directs even modest incremental volume, replacement of the $300M revenue gap becomes faster than modeled.

$90M Buyback Authorization Live

Of the $100M Feb-2025 board authorization, only $10M was used in 2025. $90M remaining = 32% of market cap. If management uses even half opportunistically below $5/share, equity returns are dramatically accelerated.

Multiple Compression is Unsustainable

20-yr median EV/EBITDA = 9x. Today: ~6x. AdaptHealth: 7.5x. Even modest re-rating to 7x on $380M 2027 EBITDA = $1.04B equity ($13.5/share). Re-rating doesn't require operational excellence — just the absence of bankruptcy.

March 2027 Refinance Is a Binary Risk

$529M maturing in March 2027 ($325M TLA + $204M RCF). Fitch downgraded to B+ Negative Watch citing exactly this. If credit conditions worsen in late 2026 and the refinance happens at SOFR+800 instead of SOFR+450, interest expense jumps $20M, EBITDA coverage compresses, and equity becomes toxic to long-only money.

$300M Lost Contract Creates 12% Revenue Hole — Multi-Year Recovery

Capitated contract terminated by major commercial payor. 2026 revenue down 6%. Additional $40M bleed into 2027. Fitch warns full replacement "could take multiple years" given competitive landscape. If Accendra can't backfill volume, EBITDA guide proves optimistic.

GLP-1 Disruption to the Crown Jewel

27% of revenue is sleep therapy. Tirzepatide FDA approved for OSA Dec-2024. Komodo data: 83% reduction in CPAP starts among GLP-1 users. Even if the resupply base holds, new starts represent 20%+ of franchise. Long-duration headwind that compounds; not a 2026/27 issue but a 2028+ valuation cap.

CMS DMEPOS Bidding Reset Hits Core Categories

Late-2025 final rule: CGMs + insulin pumps reclassified to monthly rental, added to bid categories. Bidding late 2026, rates effective Jan 2028. Diabetes is 29% of revenue. Prior bid rounds slashed some categories ~50%. Even half that hit on diabetes is $115M of revenue and ~$50M EBITDA.

Diversification Loss = Single-Channel Vulnerability

Former O&M GVP: by abandoning hospital distribution, Accendra is locked out of primary vendor agreements with IDNs (Mayo, etc.). Mega-distributors (Medline, Cardinal, McKesson) bundle acute-care contracts with proprietary private-label home-care products to push DMEs out of those accounts entirely.

Compensation Misalignment

Executive comp tied to adj. EBITDA, adj. operating income, revenue growth — not free cash flow. For a leveraged turnaround where FCF is the only thing that matters, this is a flagged misalignment. Adjusted metrics can look clean while cash leaks. DV Capital flagged this risk in the original thesis.

Margin Compression vs Diversified Distributors

Pure-play home-care margins ~10-13%. Medline, Cardinal achieve high-teens via acute-care bundling and scale on private-label. Without acute footprint, Accendra structurally ceiling'd at low-mid teens unless tech leverage delivers (unproven at scale).

Take-Private at Premium Caps Upside

At $281M mkt cap, a PE buyer (Platinum, Apollo) could offer $5-7 per share — premium to today, fraction of the bull case. Board may accept. Doug Springer in DV Capital comment thread already noting bond strategy implies someone views ACH as restructuring candidate, not turnaround.

11Key Risks & What Would Break the Thesis

Honest accounting of what can go wrong. The DV Capital thesis explicitly flags six material risks. AlphaSense identifies refinancing as the binary downgrade trigger.

1. Refinancing Wall Failure

What: March 2027 RCF + TLA refi at penalty rates or fails altogether.

Probability: Moderate. Bond market for B+ healthcare names depends heavily on macro. Rate environment 2H26 critical.

Impact: Equity-killer. Forces dilutive raise, distressed restructuring, or sale.

Watchlist: Q3-2026 earnings color on refi process. Deutsche Bank/Fitch credit commentary.

2. Stranded Costs Linger

What: $36.5M stranded costs phase down slower than guided.

Probability: Moderate. IT/HR system rationalization always slips.

Impact: 2027 EBITDA missed by $15-25M, FCF below $100M, leverage stuck above 5x.

Watchlist: 1H 2026 earnings — must show measurable cost reduction quarter-over-quarter.

3. Additional Contract Losses

What: Another commercial payer cancels, replicating the $300M hole.

Probability: Low-moderate. The lost contract was a competitively bid capitation deal — not core fee-for-service.

Impact: Cumulative revenue erosion, EBITDA leverage worsens, multiple compresses.

Watchlist: Quarterly contract win/loss disclosures.

4. CMS Competitive Bidding Severity

What: Jan 2028 rates cut diabetes/ostomy by 30-50% as in prior rounds.

Probability: Moderate. CMS posture is tougher this cycle.

Impact: Beyond 2027 model. ~$50-75M annual EBITDA at risk if worst case. Offset by volume share gains from smaller players exiting.

Watchlist: Late-2026 bid submissions, mid-2027 award announcements.

5. GLP-1 Adoption Curve

What: Tirzepatide for OSA + future indications + supply normalization shrink CPAP TAM faster than expected.

Probability: High over 5+ years; low for 2026-2027 numbers.

Impact: Long-duration headwind, multiple cap. Apria revenue could be 10-15% lower by 2030 than current trajectory implies.

Watchlist: Quarterly new-CPAP-start metrics, Eli Lilly Zepbound script trends.

6. Take-Private at Discount

What: PE bidder offers $5-7/share premium; board accepts to crystallize value.

Probability: Moderate. Distressed PE shops circle this kind of asset.

Impact: Caps upside at 50-100% rather than 250-400%. Premature value realization.

Watchlist: Unusual options activity, 13D/G filings, board changes.

7. Equity Dilution

What: If FCF disappoints AND refi looks shaky, mgmt issues equity to plug gap.

Probability: Low — would be capitulation. Mgmt clearly avoiding.

Impact: At $3-4 share, even $200M raise = 50%+ dilution. Catastrophic.

Watchlist: Shelf registration filings, S-3 activity.

8. Manufacturer Concentration / Cost Inflation

What: Top-3 suppliers = 40% of purchases. Cost increases 2026 already cited as headwind.

Probability: Continues. ResMed et al have pricing power.

Impact: 50-100bps margin compression annually if unchecked.

Watchlist: Gross margin Q-over-Q. Manufacturer earnings calls.

What Breaks the Thesis Outright

The thesis is broken if any one of these happens:

  1. March 2027 refinance fails or comes at SOFR+800+. Equity goes to zero or near-zero in restructuring scenarios.
  2. 1H 2026 earnings show stranded costs flat or rising. Indicates cost-out program isn't real — destroys 2027 EBITDA bridge.
  3. Equity raise announced. 50%+ dilution at current price levels permanently impairs returns.
  4. Second major contract loss in 2026. Demonstrates churn isn't an isolated event; revenue base is shrinking, not stabilizing.

Conversely, if H1-2026 prints in line with EBITDA guide ($335-355M), stranded cost run-rate is below $25M annualized exiting Q4 2026, and refi indications come at SOFR+450 or below — the bull case is essentially confirmed and equity should re-rate hard.
